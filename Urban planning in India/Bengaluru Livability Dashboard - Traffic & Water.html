Air quality — PM2.5 vs the limits NEW · wired via data pipeline

Monthly-average PM2.5 (µg/m³), December 2024. Even Bengaluru — among India's cleaner big cities — runs well above the WHO guideline, though below India's own lax standard.

India's annual PM2.5 standard is 40 µg/m³; the WHO guideline is just 5. Bengaluru (~34) clears the Indian bar but is ~7× the WHO level — Mumbai and Kolkata are far worse.

Source: Statista — monthly PM2.5 in Indian cities (Dec 2024) · live-update ready via OpenAQ / WAQI.

What the numbers say

Bengaluru's congestion is structural: 1.23 crore vehicles on a road network that delivers ~17.6 km/h in the core, costing the city ~₹20,000 crore a year. Yet 35.8 lakh people a day already choose BMTC buses — more than 5× the metro. The cheapest win is not more flyovers; it is faster, more frequent buses and safe footpaths.

What the numbers say

Bengaluru pumps Cauvery water ~90 km uphill to deliver barely the benchmark, while its own rainfall and lakes go uncaptured and its groundwater is over-extracted. On treatment it is actually a national leader — but capacity on paper isn't the same as treated water in the lake. The fight is now about operation, reuse and accountability, not just pipes.

Groundwater — drawn nearly twice as fast as it recharges NEW · wired via data pipeline

Index where 100 = extraction equals annual recharge (sustainable). Bengaluru draws far beyond that — every urban groundwater unit is classified "over-exploited" (CGWB 2023).

Over the safe limit and past the sustainable line: Bengaluru extracts roughly 1.9× its natural recharge, a core driver of the 2024 water crisis.

Source: OpenCity · CGWB 2023 assessment (reporting).

From data to debate to decisions

A dashboard only matters if it changes something. Here is how a citizen, an RWA, or a civic group can use the figures above to engage the government.

The headline case

Bengaluru loses ~34 minutes to move 10 km, buries ~893 people a year in road crashes, leaks ~1.9× its groundwater recharge, and adds 7.22 lakh vehicles a year — at a congestion cost of ~₹20,000 crore. These are not acts of nature; they are budget and design choices that can be debated and changed.

Mobility · Ask

Fund footpaths before flyovers

Pedestrians are a large share of road deaths. Demand a published, ward-wise footpath & safe-crossing budget and a Vision-Zero target in the city's annual plan.

Mobility · Ask

Bus priority, not just metro

35.8 lakh ride BMTC daily — 5× the metro. Push for bus-priority lanes and frequency targets; track them publicly each quarter.

Water · Ask

Treated-water accountability

Capacity ≠ treatment. Demand monthly, plant-wise STP performance data and lake-quality reporting in the open.

Water · Ask

Capture local water first

Before more Cauvery is pumped 90 km uphill, demand rainwater-harvesting enforcement, lake rejuvenation and treated-water reuse targets.

Process · Ask

Make the data routine & open

Ask BBMP/BWSSB/BTP to publish these indicators on a fixed schedule in machine-readable form, so debate runs on facts.

Process · Ask

Benchmark against peers

Chennai cut road deaths sharply with focused intervention. Use peer cities as proof improvement is possible — and a yardstick for officials.

Where to get the underlying data

OpenCity (Oorvani Foundation) publishes Bengaluru & Indian-city datasets under open licences — including the Bengaluru Traffic Police road-accident data used here. CPCB, MoRTH, CGWB, NITI Aayog, MoHUA and BWSSB are the primary upstream sources.
