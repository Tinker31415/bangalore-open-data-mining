The bottom line

Bengaluru runs India's best bus system — largest fleet, highest utilisation, lowest losses — yet it has given those buses no dedicated road space, while a 96 km metro spine struggles to cover a sprawling 1.4-crore city. Public-transport mode share (~32%) is slipping as private vehicles surge. The highest-impact, lowest-cost move — proven in Jakarta and Bogotá — is Bus Rapid Transit, at roughly one-fortieth the cost per km of metro. And the free-bus debate, properly read, is not "free vs paid" but who funds the subsidy and whether service investment is protected.

What the numbers say

Namma Metro's per-km productivity is healthy, so the constraint is reach, not demand. At 96 km for a 1.4-crore metropolis, most of the city is still beyond walking distance of a station. The fix is faster Phase 2/3 build-out plus strong feeder buses — a station 4 km from home isn't a usable station.

Genuinely India's best bus system…

Largest fleet, highest utilisation (91%), lowest financial losses among India's eight big-city bus operators, and compliant with the fleet-size norm. BMTC moves ~44 lakh people a day — far more than the metro.

…but starved of road space and scale

Those buses crawl in the same gridlock as cars: Bengaluru has essentially no dedicated BRT corridor, only limited bus-priority pilots. And ~50 buses/lakh is the floor of the norm for a city this size and this fast-growing. The cheapest, fastest mobility win available is more buses on protected lanes.

The central lesson from abroad

Bengaluru runs more buses than any Indian city, yet — unlike Jakarta (251 km BRT, ~1M riders/day) or Bogotá (114 km BRT, ~2.3M riders/day) — it has never given those buses their own road. Bus Rapid Transit is the highest-impact, lowest-cost, fastest-to-build move in the world's most successful comparable cities, and the biggest gap in Bengaluru's plan.

The financing reality

Metros are debt-financed: BMRCL earns an operating profit but posts net losses because interest and depreciation dominate. At ~₹541 cr/km, metro can only ever be the spine. The affordable, fast-to-build muscle around it — feeder buses and BRT — costs 20–40× less per km, yet attracts a fraction of the political attention and capital.

Exhibit 14 · The free-bus question

Affordability at the end-user level — both sides of the debate

Bengaluru's mobility politics now centres on the Karnataka Shakti scheme — free travel for women on ordinary buses since June 2023. Because this is genuinely contested, here is the case on each side, then what the data shows.

The case for free / subsidised travel

For a low-income worker, a daily round-trip can eat 5–10% of the day's wage — a real barrier to taking a job at all. Removing it expands access: supporters cite a reported 23% rise in women's employment in Bengaluru, 500+ crore free rides, and even ₹300+ cr of extra GST from induced spending. Buses are the most progressive mode — those who depend on them have the fewest alternatives.

The case against (as critics put it)

Critics call it "revdi" (freebie) politics: it isn't means-tested, so wealthy women ride free while men — including poor men — pay full, now-hiked fares. They argue the money would do more good buying buses and raising frequency, and warn that subsidies which crowd out investment leave everyone with a worse, more crowded service.

These are the positions as their advocates would put them; the framing of the "against" case reflects critics' arguments, not this review's view.

Exhibit 16 · Reading

What the data supports — and what it doesn't

A grounded reading that cuts across the politics:

  • Free travel has not bankrupted BMTC: net losses are the lowest since COVID; traffic revenue is back to pre-pandemic levels.
  • But part of the bill is paid by a 15% fare hike (Jan 2025) on everyone else — felt hardest by the ~40 lakh low-income daily riders.
  • The real failure is under-investment: ~25% fewer routes and ~20% fewer effective km than 2014–15; CAG found 64% of buses run just one schedule.
  • So the honest debate isn't "free vs paid" — it's "who funds the subsidy, and is service investment protected?"
A financing design that squares the circle

Keep fares affordable and grow the system by separating the two budgets: (1) fund concessions explicitly from the state budget as a transparent line item — not out of BMTC's farebox or capital; (2) ring-fence investment via a dedicated Urban Transport Fund; (3) raise non-fare revenue — land value capture and TOD around stations, advertising, recaptured GST/parking. Affordability becomes a funded policy choice, not a hidden tax on the next commuter.

A phased plan to lift mode share and ease congestion

The one target that ties it together

Adopt a binding, time-bound goal — 50%+ of trips by public & non-motorised transport — the way Jakarta committed to 60% by 2029. Publish progress on an open dashboard so citizens and the press can hold the city to it.

Grounding

Anchored in initiatives already under way

The roadmap builds on things already legislated or in motion: Metro Phase 2/3; the Bengaluru Suburban Rail Project (148 km, ₹15,767 cr via K-RIDE — 54.9 km under construction, delayed to ~2027 on land issues); the BMLTA unified-transport-authority law; rapid bus electrification (1,568 e-buses); and the Karnataka Shakti free-bus scheme that has boosted ridership. The gap is execution speed, road-space allocation, fare integration and a binding mode-share target.
